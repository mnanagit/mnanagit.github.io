About Me

I am a Research Assistant at ETH Zürich (NADEL) and a graduate of the Paris School of Economics, where I completed the M2 in Analysis and Policy in Economics with Distinction. I also hold a Master’s degree in Mathematical Economics and Statistics from the African School of Economics. My research interests span development economics, political economy, labor economics, and environmental economics, with a particular focus on understanding how institutions, conflict, inequality, and climate shocks shape economic outcomes in low- and middle-income countries. I am broadly interested in applying modern econometric methods to identify causal mechanisms and generate evidence that can inform public policy.

Working Papers

Climate Shocks and the Distribution of Income and Wealth: Evidence from 159 Countries, 1980–2023

Mohamed Nana, Philipp Bothe, Lucas Chancel

World Inequality Lab Technical Note 2026/01.

Technical Note

We study the effects of temperature shocks on the distribution of income and wealth across 159 countries over 1980–2023. Combining distributional data from the World Inequality Database with disaggregated daily climate records, panel regressions with country and year fixed effects reveal a nonlinear relationship: extreme heat (days above 30°C) significantly reduces income growth by 0.06–0.07 percentage points per additional day. The damage pattern is remarkably uniform across the income distribution, with no statistically distinguishable differences between deciles.

Military Empowerment and Internal Security: Evidence from Regime Transition in Burkina Faso

Mohamed Nana

Master's thesis, Paris School of Economics, 2025. Supervisor: Prof. Olivier Vanden Eynde

Working Paper

This paper investigates the impact of regime change and subsequent military empowerment on internal conflict dynamics in Burkina Faso. Using a difference-in-differences design and event study analysis at the commune level, I examine variations in violence before and after the January 2022 military coup, conditional on proximity to national security force bases. Enhanced military capabilities led to a significant reduction in violent events, most pronounced in communes located further from military installations — suggesting that empowerment extended operational reach to previously underserved areas.

Healthy Diets in Eight West African Countries: Access and Preferences

Rachel Gilbert, Hervé Guéné, Harounan Kazianga, William Masters, Mohamed Nana

Preliminary — do not cite without permission. 2025.

Under Review

Using data from approximately 46,000 households surveyed in 2018–19 and 2021–22, we estimate an Exact Affine Stone Index demand system for 19 goods in rural and urban areas of eight West African countries. We simulate the impact of price reductions or vouchers for locally available nutrient-rich foods compared to cash transfers. Cash transfers worth 25% of mean per-capita expenditure for the poorest quartile increase consumption of nutrient-rich items by 15–40% for vegetables, fruit, and legumes.

Not All Inequalities Are Created Equal: Neutral Public-Good Allocation and Persistent Ethnic Disparities in Burkina Faso

Harounan Kazianga, Mohamed Nana, Tade Okediji, Rodney Smith, Brice Zoungrana

Under review, 2024.

Under Review

This paper examines how historical spatial endowments, rather than explicit ethnic discrimination, have created persistent ethnic inequalities in Burkina Faso. Communes with higher historical population density received significantly more primary schools per 10 km², raising completion rates and formal labor market participation. Ethnic disparities align closely with these historical settlement patterns; controlling for educational attainment nearly eliminates ethnic employment gaps, indicating that disparities arise from unequal school access rooted in geography rather than labor market discrimination.
